# Abstract

Hypertension, hyperglycemia, and dyslipidemia—collectively known as the "three highs"—are major chronic diseases and key modifiable risk factors for cardiovascular disease. However, conventional risk assessment methods based on blood test results rely on single-timepoint data, failing to capture the dynamic health trajectories embedded in longitudinal records. Therefore, this study adopted a three-timepoint longitudinal design (Y-2, Y-1, Y0), using health indicators and their change features over time (Delta Features, Δ) to predict the status of the three conditions. A total of ten predictive models—including traditional statistics, K-Nearest Neighbors (KNN), tree-based methods, kernel methods, and neural networks—were systematically compared, with symbolic regression employed to explore the interpretability of the predictive models. The study used a publicly available longitudinal health checkup dataset (Luo et al., 2023), which comprises 6,056 community-dwelling adults aged 40 and above followed from 2010 to 2018; a sliding window approach generated 13,514 modeling records, and StratifiedGroupKFold 5-fold cross-validation was performed.

The main findings are: (1) the Logistic Regression model demonstrated stable and superior performance, achieving an Area Under the Curve (AUC) of 0.938 for hyperglycemia; (2) accumulating more checkup records improved prediction performance, with AUC positively correlated with checkup frequency (e.g., hypertension AUC increased from 0.676 to 0.835); (3) SHapley Additive exPlanations (SHAP) revealed disease-specific predictors; (4) symbolic regression discovered a minimal prediction formula achieving an AUC of 0.943; (5) using only the top 2 most important features, the AUC decrease remained within only 1.5%. In conclusion, this study demonstrates that longitudinal health checkup data, through simple feature engineering and linear models, can achieve clinically useful predictions, suitable for early warning systems in primary healthcare units.

**Keywords**: Hypertension, Hyperglycemia, Dyslipidemia, Machine Learning, Delta Features
