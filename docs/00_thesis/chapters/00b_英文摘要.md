# Abstract

Hypertension, hyperglycemia, and dyslipidemia—collectively known as the "three highs"—are major chronic diseases and key modifiable risk factors for cardiovascular disease. However, conventional risk assessment methods based on blood test results rely on single-timepoint data, failing to capture the dynamic health trajectories embedded in longitudinal records.

Therefore, this study adopted a three-timepoint longitudinal design (Y-2, Y-1, Y0), using health indicators and their change features (Delta Features, Δ) to predict the status of the three conditions. Ten predictive models (traditional statistics, KNN, tree-based, kernel methods, and neural network) were systematically compared, with symbolic regression employed to explore model interpretability. The study used a publicly available longitudinal health checkup dataset (Luo et al., 2023), comprising 6,056 community-dwelling adults aged 40 and above, followed from 2010 to 2018, with a sliding window approach generating 13,514 modeling records, and StratifiedGroupKFold 5-fold cross-validation was applied.

Key findings: (1) the Logistic Regression model demonstrated stable and superior performance, achieving an Area Under the Curve (AUC) of 0.938 for hyperglycemia; (2) accumulating more checkup records improved prediction performance, with AUC positively correlated with checkup frequency (e.g., hypertension AUC increased from 0.676 to 0.835); (3) SHapley Additive exPlanations (SHAP) revealed disease-specific predictors; (4) symbolic regression discovered a minimal prediction formula achieving an AUC of 0.943; (5) using only the top 2 most important features, the AUC decrease remained within 1.5%. This study demonstrates that longitudinal health checkup data, through simple feature engineering and linear models, can achieve clinically useful predictions, suitable for early warning systems in primary healthcare units.

**Keywords**: Hypertension, Hyperglycemia, Dyslipidemia, Machine Learning, Delta Features
